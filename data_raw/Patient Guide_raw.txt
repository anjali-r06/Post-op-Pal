Patient Guide: Recovering from an Appendectomy  
Post -Operative Care & Discharge Instructions  
 
1. Introduction  
You have undergone an appendectomy (surgical removal of the appendix). Whether you had a 
Laparoscopic  surgery (small incisions) or Open  surgery (one larger incision), your body needs time to 
heal. Most patients recover fully within 2 to 6 weeks.  
This guide outlines how to care for yourself at home to ensure a safe and speedy recovery.  
 
2. Incision & Wound Care  
Proper wound care minimizes scarring and prevents infection.  
• Keep it Clean and Dry:  You may usually shower 24 to 48 hours after surgery (unless told 
otherwise). Wash the incision area gently with mild soap and water. Pat dry with a clean 
towel —do not rub.  
• Dressings & Bandages:  If you have a dressing over the incision, change it as instructed by 
your surgeon. If you have Steri -Strips  (small tape strips) or medical glue, let them fall off on 
their own (usually in 7 –10 days).  
• Restrictions:  Do not soak in a bathtub, swimming pool, or hot tub until your doctor confirms 
the wound is fully closed (usually 2 weeks).  
• Clothing:  Wear loose -fitting clothing that does not rub against your incision site or waistline.  
 
3. Pain Management  
It is normal to feel soreness around the incision and deep inside the abdomen.  
• Medication:  Take pain medication exactly as prescribed. If prescribed narcotics/opioids, take 
them only when pain is severe. Transition to over -the-counter pain relievers (like 
Acetaminophen/Paracetamol or Ibuprofen) as the pain lessens.  
• Gas Pain (Laparoscopic Surgery):  You may feel sharp pain in your shoulder or under your 
ribs. This is caused by the gas used to inflate your abdomen during surgery. Walking helps 
the body absorb this gas.  
• Splinting:  Hold a pillow firmly against your abdomen when you cough, sneeze, laugh, or take 
a deep breath to support your muscles and reduce pain.  
 
4. Diet & Digestion  
Your digestive system may be slow to "wake up" after anesthesia.  
• Start Slow:  Begin with clear liquids (broth, water, juice). If you tolerate these well, move to 
bland foods (crackers, toast, rice), and finally back to your normal diet.  
• Hydration:  Drink 8 –10 glasses of water daily to prevent dehydration and help flush out 
medications.  
• Constipation:  Pain medications and inactivity can cause constipation. Increase your fiber 
intake (fruits, vegetables, whole grains) or use a stool softener if recommended by your 
doctor. Do not strain during bowel movements.  
 
5. Activity & Rest  
Balancing rest with light movement is key to preventing blood clots and pneumonia.  
• Walking:  Get up and walk for short periods (5 –10 minutes) every few hours while awake. 
This promotes blood flow and bowel function.  
• Lifting Restrictions:  Do not lift anything heavier than 10 pounds (4.5 kg)  (roughly the weight 
of a gallon of milk) for 2 to 4 weeks, or until cleared by your doctor.  
• Driving:  Do not drive while taking narcotic pain medication. You must also be able to stomp 
on the brake pedal without hesitation or pain before driving (usually 1 –2 weeks).  
• Work/School:  Most people return to desk work or school in 1 to 2 weeks. Physical jobs may 
require 4 to 6 weeks of recovery.  
 
6. When to Call Your Doctor  
Seek medical attention immediately if you notice any of the following warning signs:  
• Fever:  A temperature higher than 101°F (38.3°C).  
• Incision Issues:  Increased redness, swelling, warmth, opening of the wound, or foul -smelling 
drainage (pus).  
• Severe Pain:  Pain that is not relieved by your prescribed medication.  
• Digestive Issues:  Persistent nausea, vomiting, or inability to pass gas or stool for more than 3 
days.  
• Circulation Issues:  Swelling, pain, or redness in one of your legs (signs of a blood clot).  
• Breathing:  Shortness of breath or chest pain.  
 
7. Follow -Up Appointment  
You likely have a follow -up appointment scheduled for 1 to 2 weeks  after your surgery. During this 
visit, the doctor will check your incision and remove any non -dissolvable stitches or staples.  
Note:  This document is for informational purposes only and does not replace the specific advice of 
your surgeon. Always follow the discharge papers provided by your hospital.
